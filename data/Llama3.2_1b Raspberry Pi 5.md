## Prompt
```
Tell me about the speed of light.
```

## Response
```
The speed of light is a fundamental constant in physics that plays a crucial role in understanding the behavior of
matter and energy. It's estimated to be approximately 299,792,458 meters per second (m/s) or about 186,282 miles
per second.

This universal speed limit is known as the speed of light in vacuum, denoted by the letter c. It's a fundamental
constant that remains unchanged regardless of the observer's relative motion or the medium through which the light
travels.

The reason for this speed limit is based on the theory of special relativity proposed by Albert Einstein in 1905
and generalized to general relativity in 1915. According to this theory, time and space are intertwined as a
single entity called spacetime, and objects with mass or energy must follow the curvature of spacetime.

In the case of light, its speed is determined by the properties of photons, which are particles that make up
light. Photons have zero rest mass (the mass of an object at rest) but do have momentum, which they carry away
from any moving object as the wave function decays into a plane wave.

The speed of light is so fast because it's essentially a "cosmic speed limit" imposed by the laws of physics
themselves. It sets a universal boundary beyond which no other object can travel faster than a certain distance
per second.

This speed limit has been experimentally verified countless times, from high-speed particle accelerators to
astronomical observations of light and matter in various environments.

Some mind-bending facts about the speed of light include:

* When you look at the night sky, the stars appear to be moving away from you due to their light traveling long
distances through space. This is because the speed of light has been traveling for billions of years since it was
emitted.
* If you were to travel close to the speed of light (about 300,000 km/s), time would appear to slow down relative
to outside observers. However, your clock would still tick at its normal rate within a given frame of reference.
* As you approach even faster speeds (e.g., near-lightspeed travel in a hypothetical warp drive), particles and
objects will start experiencing time dilation effects due to their proximity to the speed of light.

The speed of light remains an fundamental constant that underlies our understanding of physics, and its
implications continue to inspire scientific investigation and philosophical discussions about space, time, and
matter.
```
